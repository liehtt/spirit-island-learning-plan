01CORE RULESOperating Rules (Global Constraints)

These five rules apply to every game in the plan. Violating them produces unreliable learning data — false-mastery readings, burnout, swingy losses you can't diagnose.

1. Complexity Gate [non-negotiable]

Stay on tier
  • Low spirits (8 available): Lightning, River, Shadows, Vital Strength, plus all 5 Horizons spirits
  • Moderate spirits: only after 5+ clean Low-complexity wins
  • High spirits: only after 5+ clean Moderate wins at adversary L2+
  • Very High: only after 10+ High-complexity games — these spirits punish under-prepared players hard
Why: the official complexity rating reflects how many board states you need to evaluate per turn. Skipping a tier means you'll play the spirit incorrectly and learn the wrong lessons.

2. Session Limits & Burnout Prevention [non-negotiable]

Session caps
  • Max 2 single-spirit games per session, or 1 multi-handed game
  • Max 1 game per session if you're playing Jagged Earth + Adversary L4+ (2-hour cognitive load minimum)
  • Hard stop after 2 consecutive games — even if you have time
  • Stop on hot streaks too — fatigue inflates wins. The streak is the worst time to keep playing.
  • Take a 5-minute break between games — physical movement, water, look away from the screen
Burnout symptoms — STOP if you notice these
  • Drafting on autopilot — picking the first Minor without reading the others
  • "Just one more turn" syndrome — refusing to acknowledge an impending loss
  • Forgetting Innate thresholds turn after turn (the #1 fatigue signal)
  • Decision inflation — 5+ minutes to draft when you usually take 30 seconds
  • Frustration response — annoyed at an Invader card flip, not analyzing it
  • Can't articulate why you played a card after the turn ends
  • Undo-spam — repeatedly undoing past randomness reveals (cheating yourself out of learning)
Recovery actions if symptoms appear
  • Resign the current game immediately. Don't finish "to see how it ends" — that's sunk-cost, not learning.
  • Take 24–48 hours off from Spirit Island. Other games are fine; this one needs sleep to settle.
  • On return, run one Quick Play → Beginner game to reset feel (no adversary, familiar Low spirit, Power Progression)
  • If symptoms persist for 2 sessions: drop a complexity layer — lower adversary level, switch to a simpler spirit, or revert one Phase
Why session limits matter more in Spirit Island than most games: a single complex turn involves drafting from a 4-card pool, evaluating innate thresholds, planning Slow/Fast resolution order, and predicting 2 turns of Invader cards. After 90 minutes, your working memory degrades silently. You'll think you played well; the game record will say otherwise.

3. Spirit Rotation Rule

  • No spirit twice in a row at the same setup. Vary spirit, adversary, or board.
  • Maintain exposure diversity. Playing 5 River games in a row teaches River — not Spirit Island.

4. Regression Trigger

If you experience
  • Unclear losses — "I don't know why I lost" — the diagnostic gate failing silently
  • Cognitive overflow — too many tokens, presence, powers to track at once
  • Power-draft paralysis — taking > 90 seconds to draft a minor

Action: drop the latest complexity layer (e.g., remove the adversary, drop to single-spirit, revert to a Low spirit), and replay the previous phase.

5. Energy & Elements Awareness

Always
  • Never end a turn with unspent Energy — Energy doesn't snowball, only spend does.
  • Check your innate-power thresholds every turn. Innate powers are "free extra cards per turn" — you're already paying for them in elements.
  • Look at the invader board BEFORE the spirit phase. Plan around Build & Ravage timing, not after.

02PHASE 0Orientation — Learn the Loop (~3–5 games)

Goal: internalize the Invader sequence (Explore → Build → Ravage), the Spirit phase rhythm, Dahan behavior, Blight cards, and the Fear track. Don't try to "win" yet — observe the engine running.

Setup

  • Run the in-app Tutorial first — Handelabra's tutorial introduces concepts with hand-holding, then lets you finish a real game.
  • Then run Quick Play → Beginner mode 3–4 times. This is randomized: 1 Low-complexity spirit, no adversary, no Blight card variant, no scenario.
  • Use 2D Classic map view initially — less visual noise than the 3D textured view.
  • Adversary: none. You're learning the base engine, not fighting yet.

The Invader Sequence — memorize this

Explore
Invader cards from current stage flip; Explorers spawn into matching terrains and adjacent-to-cities/towns.
Cheapest to stop. Block the spawn → no Build next turn.
Build
Lands containing 2+ Explorers/Towns get a Town. 2+ Towns get a City.
Removing Explorers BEFORE Build is your strongest non-damage play.
Ravage
Invaders attack: 2 damage per Explorer, 2 per Town, 3 per City. If damage ≥ land health → Blight + Dahan/Presence destroyed.
Slow powers don't prevent THIS ravage — they affect next turn's. Use Fast powers to defend now.
The pre-emption rule: kill Explorers (cheap) > remove Towns (mid) > defend during Ravage (expensive, lossy). The earlier in the sequence you can intervene, the better the ratio.

Learning targets (no scoreboard, just observation)

  • Spirit phase: Growth → Energy → Cards (Plays + Fasts/Slows) → Resolve actions
  • Slow vs Fast: Fast resolves before invaders that turn, Slow resolves after. Memorize this — it's the #1 newbie pitfall.
  • Element thresholds: tap your spirit's Innate Powers; see what elements you generate naturally
  • Blight cards: watch the cascading Blight ratio — it spreads to ONE adjacent land per Blight added, not all neighbors
  • Fear track: when Terror Level rises, victory conditions get easier (eventually you only need to destroy Cities)

Use the digital advantages

Digital perks
  • Undo liberally in this phase — the digital game's main undo and Spirit-phase granular undo let you experiment with sequencing without penalty
  • Tap any token/power for an in-game explanation — the rule reference is one click away
  • Watch the invader preview — digital surfaces what the next 2 turns of explore/build/ravage will affect
Diagnostic Checkpoint 0 — must answer without checking
  • What is the order: Explore / Build / Ravage — and what triggers each?
  • What's the difference between Fast and Slow powers, and when does each resolve relative to the Invaders?
  • What causes Blight to cascade, and to how many lands?
  • What are the two loss conditions? (Blight card flips with no Blight in supply, OR all Spirits' Presence destroyed.)
  • What are the two win conditions? (Terror Level 3 with no Cities, then Towns, etc., OR Terror Level 4.)
Fail: Re-play 1 game with the rules reference open. Re-attempt.
Pass: proceed to Phase 1.

03PHASE 1Power Progression — One Spirit at a Time (~6–8 games)

Now you play to win, but on training wheels. Each game you use the Power Progression card — predetermined minor/major draws that ensure a coherent build. This removes draft pressure so you can focus on using your spirit.

Why Power Progression: drafting and learning a spirit at the same time is too much. Power Progression hands you the curated archetype the designers built. Run the rails until the spirit's logic clicks, then take the wheel.

Setup (every game)

  • Single spirit, single board (true solo). Pick board A or B — most balanced.
  • Use the Power Progression setup option in digital ("Use predetermined Powers" in custom game)
  • No adversary, no scenario, no Blight card variant
  • The Difficulty rating shown should be 0

Recommended spirit order (do all 4 in this order)

Game 1–2
River Surges in Sunlight
  • Elements: SUNWATEREARTHPLANT
  • Learns: Gather + Push (movement is your damage prevention)
  • Easiest "engine" to feel because growth is forgiving
Game 3–4
Vital Strength of the Earth
  • Elements: SUNFIREEARTHPLANT
  • Learns: Defend + Major Power scaling
  • Teaches the Defend mechanic explicitly
Game 5–6
Shadows Flicker Like Flame
  • Elements: MOONFIREAIR
  • Learns: Fear generation and cheap energy plays
  • The "Fear-vs-damage" decision becomes visceral here
Game 7–8
Lightning's Swift Strike
  • Elements: SUNAIRFIRE
  • Learns: Speed conversion (Slow Becomes Fast Innate)
  • Save for last — its Innate teaches a bad timing instinct if you start here
Common consensus order: River OR Vital Strength first, Lightning last. Lightning is mechanically simple but its "Slow Becomes Fast" innate teaches you to think of Slow powers as Fast — which is a habit you'll have to un-learn for every other spirit. Start with a spirit that doesn't change phase timing.

Per-game focus (do one each game)

  • Pick one element threshold on your innate panel and aim to hit it every turn
  • Pick one land as your "anchor" — predict what the Invaders will do there 2 turns out
  • Track Energy efficiency: 0–1 unspent at turn end = good; 3+ = wasted plays

System Debug — when something feels broken

Use the digital advantage When you don't understand what just happened, undo back to the start of that phase. Look at the invader card. Read the Build/Ravage rules. Re-play, observe what's different. Timebox: 5 minutes per debug. Don't try to "play perfectly" — just understand the mechanic.
Diagnostic Checkpoint 1 — must demonstrate
  • Win at least 1 game with each of the 4 Low base spirits using Power Progression (Difficulty 0)
  • Articulate each spirit's win condition: how does THIS spirit generate fear or stop invaders?
  • Innate fluency: name one threshold on each spirit and what activates it
  • Slow vs Fast: correctly identify when to play Slow defends vs Fast defends in a same-turn dilemma
  • Element tracking: hit a 2-element innate threshold deliberately, not by accident
Fail: repeat the spirit you struggle most with, then re-attempt.
Pass: proceed to Phase 2.

04PHASE 2Drafting + First Adversary (~4–6 games)

Goal: remove Power Progression, learn to draft your own minor/major powers, and add light Invader pressure with Brandenburg-Prussia Level 1 — the easiest adversary because its escalation is mostly setup-only.

Setup

  • Single spirit (your favorite from Phase 1)
  • Drop Power Progression — draft your own minors
  • Adversary: Brandenburg-Prussia Level 1 (Difficulty 2)
  • Same board, no scenario yet

Drafting heuristics

Draft cheat-sheet
  • Take Minors that match your innate elements — every match-up makes innates easier to fire
  • Reach matters: if you can't deliver a Minor's effect to a useful land, the card is dead
  • Major Powers cost a card discard. Don't draft a Major until you have 3+ cards in hand and a clear target
  • Speed: 1 Fast :: 2 Slow is a healthy ratio. Pure Slow decks lose to early Ravages

Brandenburg-Prussia L1 — what changes

  • Setup: extra Town in a coastal land
  • Most L1 effects are "ramp" — Stage III triggers earlier in the deck
  • The pace feels similar to no-adversary, just slightly faster
Why Prussia first: Prussia's escalation doesn't add new tokens or rules — it just spawns more invaders faster. You're still playing the base game, just on slightly-harder difficulty. Compare to England L1 which adds fortifications and changes win conditions.

Structured experiments — one per game

Fear Run
Spirit: Shadows or Lightning
Try to win on Terror, not destruction. Track when Fear cards flip; aim to push the Terror Level twice.
Damage Run
Spirit: Vital Strength or Lightning
Win by destroying every City. Track Energy efficiency — how many turns did you bank a Major?
Control Run
Spirit: River
Win without ever taking damage in any land. Force every invader to Explore into empty lands.

Adversary progression after winning L1 twice

  • Win L1 twice in a row → bump to Prussia Level 2
  • Win L2 twice → bump to L3, OR add a different L1 adversary (Sweden)
  • Don't grind Prussia to L6. Variety teaches more than depth.
Diagnostic Checkpoint 2 — must demonstrate
  • Win without Power Progression as 2 of the 4 Low base spirits at Prussia L1
  • Draft justification: after each draft pick, you can articulate in one sentence why you took it (element match / reach / speed)
  • Adversary awareness: identify Prussia's escalation moment in the deck and plan around it
  • Fear vs damage choice: read a board and choose the right victory path (don't grind to 0 Cities if you can Terror out at 3)
Fail: drop back to Power Progression for 2 games, then re-attempt.
Pass: proceed to Phase 3.

05FRAMEWORKTurn Evaluation — Problem / Blocker / Threat

The framework that defines competent play. Adapted from Fyandor's BGG strategy guide (community-validated up to Difficulty 10–11). Without this, you'll randomly react to whatever feels scary; with it, you'll consistently prioritize what matters.

The three terms

Problem
Lands in Ravage or Build slot
  • If unchecked → Blight, eventually loss
  • You can afford to let some happen — Blight is the cost of growth
  • Don't waste resources on "scary-looking" non-Problems
Threat
Condition that will cause major loss-progress
  • England: a land reaching 6+ buildings
  • France: town supply about to empty
  • Sweden: a Problem in a Dahan-rich land = brutal cascade
  • Habsburg Mining: a land approaching 7+ invader/blight density
  • Threats are rare (2–5 per game) but always engage them
Blocker
An invader piece preventing your win NOW
  • Terror 3 with all Cities destroyed except one stubborn City → that City is a Blocker
  • Terror 4 = auto-win (no Blockers possible)
  • Scan for Blockers FIRST every turn — winning is winning

The 4-step turn evaluation

  1. Identify Blockers, Threats, Problems in that order. Most turns have 0 Blockers, 0–1 Threats, 2–4 Problems.
  2. Plan around your priority. Allocate ONE power card or innate to the highest-priority thing first. Visually commit (move card to play area, mark the innate).
  3. Choose growth options to execute the plan. Need a card next turn? Reclaim. Need energy now? Take it. Already set up? Pick scaling (presence / cards).
  4. Maximize remaining card plays efficiently. Then dial back — if a Problem can wait, save the card or help a teammate.

Growth-option decision matrix

SituationPickWhy
You need a specific card next turnReclaimGet the card back
Can't afford this turn's planGain EnergyPay for the priority power
Set up, no urgent priorityAdd PresenceScaling — unlocks plays/energy/elements
Set up, hand is shallowGain CardScaling — wider future options
Blocker/Threat but no powerGain Card + (in MP) announceCritical — draft for the answer

The 4 cases of "do I have what I need?"

Case 1
Priority exists, need a card/energy
Take Reclaim or Energy growth, play the priority card.
Case 2
Priority exists, already set up
Take a scaling growth (presence/card), execute priority + maximize.
Case 3
No priority (only Problems)
Take a scaling growth. The "downturn" — let some Problems happen so you can grow.
Case 4
Priority exists, no power for it
CRITICAL. Draw a card hunting for the answer. If solo and you can't find it, accept the cost — try again next turn.

Rules of thumb (the moves that execute the framework)

Do
  • Pushing an Explorer out of a Build land = preventing 2 health of Town. Often as good as killing a Town.
  • "Resting" Towns/Cities (in non-active terrains) are NOT Problems. Don't damage them.
  • Slow powers target NEXT turn. Use Slow removal on lands just Explored (= soon-to-Build).
  • Accept some Blight. 1 Blight on a low-value land is fine if it lets you scale. Over-defending early stalls your engine.
  • Removing blight from an invader-heavy land (so it stops Exploring out) can equal killing the invaders.
Don't
  • Don't fight non-Problems. A City in a non-active terrain is irrelevant for 2+ turns.
  • Don't over-play. Playing all 3 cards every turn looks productive but stalls presence/card growth.
  • Don't try to "catch up" by removing invaders. You start behind. Grow first, crush later.
  • Don't reclaim by default. Reclaim is the weakest growth for most spirits.
Why this framework works: the invaders' actions are sequential — Explore this turn, Build next, Ravage the turn after. Each terrain takes 3 turns to fully bloom. At any moment, only 1–2 lands are real Problems. If you spend a turn fixing 3 non-Problem lands, you've gained nothing while the actual Problems progress. Triaging by Blocker → Threat → Problem keeps your attention on what matters.
Diagnostic — Framework Fluency (apply every turn)
  • At the start of every spirit phase, name the Blockers (often none), Threats (often none), Problems (usually 2–4)
  • Commit to your priority BEFORE looking at your hand — board state drives the card, not the reverse
  • Justify your growth pick in one sentence ("Reclaiming because I need Aegis next turn")
  • If you can't articulate why a card matters, don't play it — save for next turn

06SAFEGUARDSAnti-Pattern Control System

Six ways your data lies to you. If any of these appear, your training signal is contaminated — fix the root cause before continuing.

1. The Slow-as-Fast Confusion

Symptom
You played a Slow defend "just before" the Ravage, expecting it to prevent damage. The Ravage happened anyway.

Reality: Slow powers resolve AFTER the Invader phase that turn — they're for NEXT turn's setup, not this turn's defense.

Fix: Tag every power card by speed before your spirit phase. Say out loud (or in your head) "This is Slow — it affects next turn." Lightning's "Slow Becomes Fast" Innate is the only exception, and even there, the threshold must be met that turn.

2. The Vagabond Trap (Spirit Island flavor)

Symptom
You've won 3 of your last 5 games as Rampant Green and feel mastery is approaching.

Reality: A few spirits — A Spread of Rampant Green, Keeper, Lure of the Deep Wilderness — have a wide "Swiss army" toolkit that papers over poor play. They make true solo easy but don't teach you the full game.

Validation gate: mastery requires winning with a narrow-but-deep spirit (e.g., Bringer, Shadows, Heart of the Wildfire) too. If you can only win with toolkit spirits, you're not yet competent.

3. Energy Hoarding

Symptom
You ended turn 4 with 6 unspent Energy "for a Major next turn."

Reality: 6 spent Energy across turn 4 + turn 5 is almost always stronger than 6 hoarded then spent at turn 5. Plus, you risk losing Presence/Cards to a bad Ravage between then.

Fix: Default to spending Energy each turn. If a Major needs banking, hold ≤ 2 turns max.

4. Element Blindness

Symptom
You consistently fail to hit an Innate Power's threshold despite plays each turn.

Reality: Your draft is mis-aligned. You're picking damage Minors when your innate needs MOONAIR for its key effect, and your draft pool has nothing matching.

Fix: When drafting, look at your Innates FIRST. Pick cards that complete your most-used innate threshold, even if they have lower raw effects.

5. Blight Cascade Misunderstanding

Symptom
You think a big Ravage will Blight 3 lands at once.

Reality: ONE Ravage = ONE Blight added max, regardless of damage. The cascade triggers when blight is added to an already-blighted land, and cascades to ONE adjacent land. Not all neighbors.

Fix: Memorize the Blight rules. The digital version shows blight cascading explicitly — watch the animation.

6. Single-Adversary Plateau

If you're winning consistently against Prussia L4, you're not improving — you're optimizing for Prussia. Promote to a different adversary at L3-L4. England's loss condition (7+ buildings) and France's plantation mechanic force entirely different play styles. See §13.

7. The Catchup Trap

Symptom
Every turn you spend all your Energy removing 2–3 invaders, but the board keeps getting worse.

Reality: Spirits start mechanically behind invaders. You can't out-pace them via removal in the early game — invaders spawn faster than you can kill, by design. Trying to "catch up" via removal stalls your growth track, which keeps you behind forever.

Fix: Accept some early Blight. Spend 1–2 early turns on presence growth, not damage. Only after your tracks unlock more card plays (turns 3–4) should you actively remove. The game is exponential — your scaling has to come first.

8. The Reclaim Loop

Symptom
You take Reclaim 3+ turns out of 6, "to get back" key cards.

Reality: Reclaim is the weakest growth option for most spirits. Each reclaim costs you a presence-placement or card-gain, slowing your scaling.

Fix: Aim to reclaim ONCE every 4–5 turns max (when your hand actually empties). If you're reclaiming every other turn, you're either playing too many cards per turn (anti-pattern 11 below) OR you're not picking up enough new cards from gain-card growths. (Exception: Vital Strength's reclaim is genuinely strong because it places presence — that spirit can reclaim more freely.)

9. Non-Problem Pursuit

Symptom
You're spending damage on a 3-City land that's not in the Ravage or Build slot — "it's the scariest thing on the board."

Reality: "Resting" Towns and Cities (in non-active terrains) don't threaten you this turn. Only lands in the Build/Ravage queue are Problems. See the Turn Evaluation Framework (§05).

Fix: Before any damage play, check the Invader board — is this terrain about to Build or Ravage? If not, your damage is wasted; save it for active lands.

10. Effects-Over-Elements Drafting

Symptom
You drafted a 4-damage Minor with no element match instead of a 2-damage Minor that completes your innate threshold.

Reality: Innate Powers are free extra effects every turn you hit the threshold. Missing a threshold once = losing an entire card's worth of impact. That nearly always exceeds a +2 damage difference on one Minor.

Fix: Look at your innates BEFORE the draft. Identify the 2–3 elements you most need. Filter the pool to cards with those elements first; THEN evaluate effect strength among the filtered set.

11. Over-Play (Cardplay-Maxing)

Symptom
You play 3 cards every turn because "you have the card plays," even when 2 cards would suffice.

Reality: Playing every available card every turn empties your hand quickly, forces frequent Reclaims, and stalls scaling. The energy spent could fund a Major next turn instead.

Fix: Default to playing 2 cards/turn in the first 4 turns even if you can play 3. Scale up only when the board demands it. Holding a card = available next turn without reclaiming.

Bonus pattern: Tutorial Lock-in

You finished the tutorial 6 months ago and never moved past Quick Play Beginner. Quick Play is calibrated to be winnable — it teaches the loop but not depth. After 8–10 Quick Play wins, switch to Custom Game and choose your own spirit + adversary. The plateau is the point at which Quick Play stops teaching.

07GATESDiagnostic Checkpoints (Overview)

Every phase ends with a checkpoint. You must pass before advancing. Passing means honestly answering the gate questions — not "I think I get it" but "I can articulate this without hesitation."

After Phase 0
  • Invader sequence
  • Fast vs Slow timing
  • Blight cascade rules
  • Win/loss conditions
Checkpoint 1
After Phase 1
  • Win with all 4 base Lows
  • Each spirit's win condition
  • Innate threshold fluency
  • Element tracking
Checkpoint 2
After Phase 2
  • Draft justification
  • Adversary read
  • Fear vs damage path
Checkpoint 3
After each Phase 3 sub-phase
  • New token-type mastery
  • New spirit mechanics
  • Adapt drafts to new pool
Checkpoint 4
After Phase 4
  • High-complexity spirit win
  • L4 adversary win
  • Multi-spirit synergy
Checkpoint 5
After Phase 5
  • Incarna mechanic fluency
  • Habsburg Mining win
  • Aspect awareness
If you fail a checkpoint: trigger the Regression rule (§01) — revert one phase, drop the latest complexity layer, re-attempt.
If you pass: proceed. Don't over-think it. The gates exist to prevent skipping, not to gatekeep forever.

Honest self-assessment hack

Self-grading is unreliable. After each phase, write your answers to the checkpoint questions in your post-game log (§19). A week later, re-read what you wrote. If it sounds confused or contradictory, you didn't actually pass.

Digital advantage: replay your last game

The digital game tracks every action of your game. After a loss, scrub back to the inflection turn (the turn where you "felt" you lost) and ask: "What would I do differently from here?" That single question is worth more than 3 extra games of play.

09Phase 3A — Branch & Claw (~5–7 games)

Branch & Claw is the smallest expansion but adds the most learning-shaping mechanics. Add it FIRST.

10Phase 3B — Two-Handed Solo (~4–6 games)

Going from 1 spirit to 2 spirits doubles the strategic surface area. Most experienced solo players consider 2-handed the balanced default — game state is less swingy than true solo, and you learn spirit synergy.

Don't skip this. Many of the game's most beautiful interactions only happen across spirits — Lightning's "Slow Becomes Fast" on a teammate's Slow Major, Bringer's fear engine fed by a partner's damage, etc.

Setup

  • 2 spirits, 2 boards, adjacent boards (B+C or D+E)
  • You control both spirits each turn
  • Same adversary level you can reliably beat solo, dropped 1 level (e.g., if you beat Prussia L3 solo, try Prussia L2 two-handed first)
  • No scenario yet

  • Easy synergy — both Low complexity
  • Great first 2-handed game
River + Shadows

  • River pushes invaders into Shadows' fear-trap lands
  • Shadows generates the Terror, River prevents damage
  • Both Low complexity

  • Bringer wins on Fear; Rampant Green denies Builds while Fear accumulates

  • Both Branch & Claw spirits — practice their tokens together

Anti-patterns specific to 2-handed

  • "Both spirits do the same thing" — two damage dealers + 0 defenders = ravages destroy you. Diversify roles.
  • Decision paralysis — when you can't pick which spirit to play first, write the boards' invader cards down and resolve them in a fixed order. Don't optimize across both boards turn 1.
  • Ignoring shared powers — Power cards target lands, not "your" land. Read the targeting carefully; some powers can reach across boards.
  • Coast vs Inland mismatch — Ocean's Hungry Grasp only works on coastal lands. Don't pair it with another coast-dependent spirit on the same coastline.

Roles taxonomy

  • Damage: Lightning, Shadows, Volcano, Heart of the Wildfire, Vengeance
  • Defend: Vital Strength, Stone, Hearth-Vigil, Shroud
  • Control: River, Rampant Green, Fathomless Mud, Lure
  • Fear: Bringer, Shadows, Wandering Voice, Wounded Waters
  • Support/Major engine: Shifting Memory, Starlight, Finder

Healthy pair: at least one Damage or Fear spirit + one Defend or Control spirit. Avoid 2 pure Damage or 2 pure Defend.

14Practical Tactics & Growth Strategy

Concrete game-flow tactics distilled from BGG strategy threads, the wiki, and community guides. Once you have the framework (§05), these are the moves that execute it.

Growth-option ranking (for most spirits, most turns)

Growth priority — memorize this
  1. Add Presence — unlocks tracks (card plays, energy, elements). Single highest-value growth per game-long impact.
  2. Gain Card — wider draft pool, more flexibility for future turns.
  3. Gain Energy — situational; only when you need a Major or to pay an Event cost.
  4. Reclaim — weakest for most spirits. Costs you a real growth and gives back already-used cards.

Exceptions: Vital Strength of the Earth's reclaim places a presence (genuinely strong). Spirits with a single key card (Elemental Aegis, Strike the Querulous Foe) may need reclaim to repeat that card. Highly fast-stratagey spirits like Lightning thrive on energy. Always check your spirit's panel before applying the default.

Why presence beats everything: tracks reveal "any element," extra card plays, and energy per turn. A spirit with 8 presence on the board has roughly DOUBLE the throughput of the same spirit with 4. Growth options that don't unlock tracks (Energy, Reclaim) cap your ceiling.

Tactical recipes (memorize these)

Push Explorer ≈ Destroy Town
Pre-emptive Build denial
  • A lone Explorer in a soon-to-Build land creates a Town if left
  • Push it out → no Build there → no Ravage threat in 2 turns
  • 2 health of damage prevented from being added, often cheaper than killing the Town later
Push to Coast, Clear Inland
Geographic strategy
  • Explorers spawn only in coasts and adjacent to existing buildings
  • Clear inland buildings → inland lands become safe zones
  • Push remaining invaders coast-ward; deal with them via Ocean/Coastal spirits or Dahan
Slow Powers Target Next Turn
Speed-aware planning
  • Slow defends resolve AFTER this turn's Ravage
  • Use Slow removal on lands that JUST Explored (= soon-to-Build)
  • Slow defends pre-empt NEXT turn's Ravage, not this one's
Defend Dahan, Free Damage
Dahan synergy
  • Dahan attack during Ravages they survive
  • Defend a Dahan-rich land → Dahan still hit → free invader damage
  • 2 Dahan + Defend = 4 free invader damage
  • Keep Dahan alive — when they die, make sure it's not in vain
Sacrifice Hopeless Lands
Triage
  • A land with 3 Cities + 2 Towns is rarely savable in one turn
  • Let it Blight, keep cascade from spreading by maintaining clean adjacents
  • 1 wasted Blight ≠ losing the game. Stalling for growth ≫ desperate defense
Pre-emptive Slow Targeting
Anticipation
  • Stage I cycles through 4 terrains; if 2 have hit, the next 2 are coming
  • Put Presence near those upcoming-Explore lands
  • Play Slow Push speculatively on the terrain about to Build — strike before they arrive

The "elements over effects" draft rule

Always

When drafting Minor or Major Powers, element match beats raw effect size 80% of the time. A 2-damage Minor with the elements you need to fire your Innate is worth more than a 4-damage Minor with no element match.

Reason: Innate Powers are "free cards every turn you hit the threshold." Missing a threshold loses you the equivalent of one card's worth of impact — usually more than a +2 damage difference on a single Minor.

How to apply: look at your Innates BEFORE the draft. Identify which 2–3 elements you most need. Filter the draft to cards with those elements. Pick the best card from that filtered set.

"Don't over-play" — the discipline of holding back

You have 3 card plays and 3 cards in hand. The temptation is to play all 3, hit every threshold, and feel powerful. Often this is wrong.

If 2 cards address the priority and the 3rd is filler, holding the 3rd means:

  • It's available next turn (saves a Reclaim later)
  • You spend less Energy this turn (more for next turn's Major)
  • Your hand stays deeper (more drafting flexibility)

Default rule: play 2 cards per turn for the first 4 turns of the game, even if you can play 3. Scale up only when the board demands it.

The catchup illusion

Spirits start behind the invaders, mechanically. Turn 1: invaders have buildings and explorers; you have 2 presence and 1 card play. The natural impulse is to "catch up" by removing invaders. This loses. You catch up by growing — adding presence, gaining cards, hitting elements — until your throughput exceeds theirs. Then you crush them. Remove the urge to "do something about" the invader board every turn; sometimes the right move is to let two Ravages happen so you can place 3 presence and unlock 2 card plays.

Coastal vs Inland strategy quick-ref

  • Inland is your safe zone once cleared — Explore actions don't target pure-inland lands
  • Coast is the pressure valve — invaders enter via coasts; ocean spirits dominate there
  • Push direction matters — inland-to-coast removes invaders from easy-Explore zones; coast-to-inland creates new Build targets (bad)
  • Isolate a coastal land (JE keyword) to trap invaders without further spread
  • Concentrate Then Defend — pushing many invaders into one land and defending heavily is sometimes better than dealing with them piecemeal across 4 lands

If-then quick decisions

In-game
  • If you have a lot of energy AND your innates fire reliably → start picking Major Powers
  • If you can't activate your innates → you need more card plays (presence growth) or better element matches (better drafting)
  • If you keep reclaiming → focus on presence + hand size growth early instead of crushing invaders
  • If a Major Power doesn't have great elements but you must play it now → play it for the effect this turn, forget it from hand after

16Solo Play Strategy

Spirit Island solo has two flavors. Most players settle on a mix once they know both.

True Solo (1 spirit, reduced board)

When
  • Quick games (30–60 min)
  • Learning a new spirit deeply
  • You're tired and don't want to manage 4 stat panels

Solo-strong spirits (consistently top in community rankings):

  • A Spread of Rampant Green — fastest tempo, board denial. Often cited at 80%+ solo win rate among experienced players.
  • Lightning's Swift Strike — speed converts to damage, low Energy curve
  • Keeper of the Forbidden Wilds — Wilds tokens scale with the board state
  • Lure of the Deep Wilderness — Lure tokens move invaders without damage
  • Bringer of Dreams and Nightmares — Fear-only path is robust in true solo
  • Sharp Fangs Behind the Leaves — Beasts compensate for solo's narrow board
Avoid in true solo
  • Fractured Days, Starlight, Serpent — slow ramp, the single board punishes them
  • Ocean's Hungry Grasp — coast-only powers can be useless if your board is mostly inland

Two-Handed Solo (2 spirits, 2 boards)

When
  • You want a balanced, deeper game
  • You want to learn cross-spirit synergy
  • You're prepared for 90–120 min

See §10 for recommended starter pairs. Most experienced solo players consider 2-handed the default serious mode.

Three+ Handed Solo

Advanced Possible but mentally exhausting. Most players cap at 2-handed unless using the digital game's preview/undo to manage cognitive load. The digital advantage is real here — automated bookkeeping makes 3+ spirits viable, where physical play would be near-impossible to track.

Solo-specific tactics

  • Play aggressively early. Solo's "narrative arc" goes: weak early → snowball mid → coast late. Don't passively accept early Ravages — fight back from turn 1.
  • Lean on Slow powers. You fully control the order — Slow setups for next turn are cheaper than Fast emergencies.
  • Track elements every turn. No teammate to "carry" your innate threshold. Miss your Fire threshold and you're playing with half the power budget.
  • Don't hoard Energy. Solo means no one else can spend the surplus.
  • The "anchor land" trick. Pick one land you'll defend at all costs. Lose another to focus protection on the anchor. Trying to save every land = losing every land.

Digital solo advantages

  • Undo — the largest concrete advantage. You can experiment with sequencing within a turn. Don't abuse it — undoing past a Ravage to "re-roll" the outcome is cheating yourself.
  • Invader preview — digital shows the next-up invader card; physical players have to remember it
  • Element tracking — digital sums and highlights element counts; physical players track manually
  • Power reference — tap any card for full rules
  • Weekly Challenge — preset scenarios for puzzle-solving practice (no replacement for free play)

When solo plateaus

If you're winning Difficulty 6+ consistently solo: try the Daily/Weekly Challenge, online multiplayer (cross-platform), or pivot to 3-handed for a fresh cognitive load. The biggest skill ceiling in solo is "you've memorized which Minors to draft" — multiplayer breaks this by giving you novel draft pools.

20Micro-Skills (Damage / Elements / Timing)

Real skills that improve your play independent of which spirit you're using. Each is drill-able in isolation.

Damage math micro-skill

  • Memorize health pools: Explorer = 1, Town = 2, City = 3. Dahan = 2.
  • Memorize Ravage damage: Explorer = 2, Town = 2, City = 3.
  • Memorize Defend interactions: Defend X reduces ALL damage in that land by X. Stacks across spirits.
  • Net damage: For each land, compute (Total Ravage damage) − (Total Defend). If < 1, no Blight, no Dahan/Presence destroyed.
  • Excess damage doesn't carry over. 8 damage in a 2-health land kills the Town and stops; the extra 6 is wasted.

Element threshold micro-skill

  • Each spirit has 1–3 Innate Powers, each with multiple thresholds
  • Innate thresholds = free powers. Every threshold hit is "+1 card played that turn"
  • Draft for threshold, not flat damage. A Minor with the right element is often stronger than a higher-damage Minor that doesn't help your Innate
  • Track which elements you generate "naturally" via your spirit's panel (the curved-edge cards)
Element shortcuts
  • SUN = damage, fear, big effects (Vital Strength, Lightning, River)
  • MOON = fear, stealth, control (Shadows, Bringer, Wandering Voice)
  • FIRE = damage, burn, blight (Vital, Lightning, Shadows, Wildfire)
  • AIR = movement, range (Lightning, Shadows, Eyes)
  • WATER = control, push, defense (River, Ocean, Shroud)
  • EARTH = defense, isolation, big damage (Vital, Stone, Devouring Teeth)
  • PLANT = grow, control, healing (Keeper, Rampant Green, Wounded Waters)
  • ANIMAL = strife, beasts, mobility (Sharp Fangs, Many Minds, Lure)

Timing micro-skill

  • Speed reasoning: Fast = before Invaders, Slow = after. Slow can REMOVE buildings that just spawned via Build, but can't prevent the current Ravage.
  • Scoring spike anticipation: count invader card stages. Stage III approaching = Ravage in 1–2 turns wherever Stage II just was.
  • Pre-emptive removal: kill Explorers in the Explore-land NOW so they don't Build NEXT turn. Cheaper than fighting Towns later.
  • Major Power banking: hold Energy when you know a Major is coming (you scouted via Shifting Memory, or you're playing a Spirit with naturally cheap Majors). Otherwise, spend.

Presence placement micro-skill

  • Presence = your "reach" for power targeting. More Presence = wider play options.
  • Don't place Presence in lands about to Ravage — Blight destroys Presence.
  • Place Presence where you'll defend, not where you'll attack — attack-only lands burn out fast.
  • Sacred Sites (2+ Presence in a land) unlock some powers' "increase effect" clauses — read your spirit's panel carefully.

Dahan micro-skill

  • Dahan = villagers, 2 HP each, attack invaders for 2 damage when their land Ravages
  • Defend a Dahan-rich land → Dahan counterattack still hits → free damage
  • Some spirits (Thunderspeaker, Hearth-Vigil) move Dahan as units
  • Some adversaries (Sweden) target Dahan specifically

21Mastery Definition (Honest)

Set expectations honestly. Spirit Island has 30+ spirits, 8 adversaries, 11+ scenarios, and the Aspect/Incarna systems multiply combinations into the tens of thousands. "Mastery" is a long road. Define stages.

Competent (after ~30 games / completing this plan to Phase 4)

You're competent when:

  • You win consistently at Difficulty 5 with familiar spirits
  • You can articulate every spirit's win condition (even ones you haven't played)
  • You always know what the Invader phase will do BEFORE the spirit phase
  • You draft Minors with element-threshold reasoning, not raw effect size
  • You've won with at least one spirit from each complexity tier (Low/Moderate/High)

Strong (50–100 games)

  • You can win Difficulty 7–8 with any spirit in your owned pool
  • You consistently 2-hand at Difficulty 6+ with planned spirit pairings
  • You can play any Very High spirit (Fractured Days, Starlight) at Difficulty 3+
  • You can describe each adversary's "personality" without lookup
  • You've tried all Aspects and have opinions on each

Mastery (~150+ games)

  • You can beat Difficulty 9–10 (Russia L6, England L6) reliably
  • You can solo any spirit at Difficulty 7+
  • You read board state and predict 3 turns ahead
  • You can teach others without confusing them
  • You see the "shape" of the game — which spirits/adversaries are mismatched, when to leverage that

True mastery (~300+ games)

  • You contribute to community strategy discussion (BGG, wiki, forums)
  • You've completed every Weekly Challenge variant
  • You can win with handicapped setups (e.g., bad spirit / bad board / max adversary)
  • You see asymmetric matchups and adapt instantly
The honest read: finishing this plan to Phase 5 makes you competent across all current content. Mastery is a separate, longer journey. Don't confuse "I beat Habsburg Mining L2" with "I'm good at Spirit Island" — but also, don't sell yourself short. Competent Spirit Island players are rare; reach that bar and you're better than 80% of casual players.

Signals you've plateaued

  • Same win rate across 10+ games at the same difficulty
  • Drafting feels automatic — you don't consider alternative picks
  • You play the same 2–3 spirits 80% of the time
  • Fix: bump difficulty by 1, force a 5-game rotation of spirits you've never played, or take the Weekly Challenge

Repeats & proliferation — the ultra-high-difficulty meta

For Difficulty 15+

At very high difficulty (two L5–L6 adversaries combined, Difficulty 15–29), most spirits stop being individually viable. The community-validated "Cheese Team" is:

  • Serpent Slumbering Beneath the Island — late-game powerhouse, holds Elemental Aegis (defend 8+ across many lands)
  • Fractured Days Split the Sky — universal support via Slip the Flow of Time (reclaim / extra card play / make-fast for teammates)
  • Shifting Memory of Ages — element gifting via Elemental Teachings, free Majors every turn
  • + Vital Strength (Might Aspect) or Downpour Drenches the World as the 4th (for repeat support)

The strategy in one line: feed Serpent's Deep Slumber track via shared presence, hit "Serpent Wakes in Power" turn 2, then repeat Elemental Aegis to defend 6–8 lands per turn from turn 2 onward — effectively locking out the invaders.

Why this exists: at extreme difficulty, only proliferation (placing presence faster than normal) and repeats (casting Majors 2–3× in one turn) can outpace invader output. See the "A serpent's breakfast" BGG write-up of beating Difficulty 29 (Russia 6 + England 6 + extra board).

You don't need this for normal play. Most players never need to cheese Difficulty 25+; competent play at Difficulty 6–10 is more than enough. This is context for when you decide to chase the absolute ceiling.

Two patterns that recur in every high-difficulty write-up: (1) draft cards for elements first, effects second — at Difficulty 15+, hitting innate thresholds matters more than +2 damage; (2) Elemental Aegis + repeats is functionally a "soft win button" — any team that can cast it 2x/turn defends the whole island.

Terms used throughout this aid + top external resources.

Plan-specific terms

Complexity Tier
Official Greater Than Games rating of how many decisions a spirit forces per turn: Low, Moderate, High, Very High. Skipping tiers leads to mis-play.
Phase (0–5)
Sequenced learning blocks. Each phase has a goal, setup constraints, learning targets, and a diagnostic checkpoint.
Diagnostic Checkpoint
Pass/fail gate at the end of each phase. Failure triggers the regression rule (revert one phase).
Power Progression
Predetermined minor/major draws for each base Low spirit. Removes draft pressure for new players. Use in Phase 1 only.
Toolkit Spirit
Spirit with wide Swiss-army-knife answers (Rampant Green, Keeper, Lure). Easy in true solo, can paper over bad strategy. Mastery requires winning with narrow spirits too.
Anchor Land
In solo play, the land you commit to defending at all costs. Letting another land fall to save the anchor is correct play.

Game mechanical terms

Explore / Build / Ravage
The 3 phases of the Invader turn. Cards trigger one of these in each terrain type. Earlier interruption = cheaper defense.
Fast vs Slow
Power speed. Fast resolves BEFORE invaders that turn; Slow resolves AFTER. The #1 newbie pitfall: playing a Slow defense for "this turn's" Ravage.
Blight Card
Health pool of the island. Adding Blight when supply is empty = lose. Cascades to ONE adjacent land per added Blight on a blighted land.
Fear Pool / Terror Level
Fear builds toward Terror Levels 2/3/4. Each Terror Level relaxes win conditions. Level 4 = automatic win.
Innate Power
Always-on power on the spirit's panel. Activates when you meet element thresholds. Free extra power each turn you hit the threshold.
Presence
Your spirit's "tokens" on the board. Required to target lands with most powers. Lost when invaders Ravage successfully.
Sacred Site
A land with 2+ Presence. Some powers' "increase effect" clauses trigger from Sacred Sites.
Dahan
The island's native villagers. 2 HP each, attack invaders for 2 damage when their land Ravages. Many spirits interact with them.
Major / Minor Power
Power cards. Minors are cheap, Majors are powerful but cost a card discard from hand. Drafted from random pools.
Strife / Wilds / Beasts / Disease
Branch & Claw token types. Modify Invader behavior (strife = weaker attacks, disease = no Build).
Badlands
Jagged Earth token. Land with Badlands deals +1 damage from spirits' damaging powers.
Isolate
JE keyword. Isolated land/invader can't move to adjacent lands. Locks down City threats.
Aspect
Alternate stat panel for a base spirit. Same elements, different powers/innates. JE introduced them; NI multiplies them.
Incarna
Nature Incarnate mechanic. A "super presence" token representing the spirit's physical form. Can be Empowered for permanent buffs.
Empowered
An Incarna upgrade — one-way permanent buff. Costs setup but yields ongoing benefits.
Coastal vs Inland
Lands either touch the sea (Coastal) or don't (Inland). Ocean's Hungry Grasp only operates on Coastal lands. Important for spirit selection.

Quick Play (Beginner/Intermediate/Expert)
Random setup. Beginner = 1 Low spirit, no adversary. Use to learn the loop fast.

Undo (Main / Spirit Phase)
Main Undo rewinds before the spirit phase; granular Spirit-Phase Undo rewinds individual actions. Cannot undo past randomness reveals.
Weekly Challenge
